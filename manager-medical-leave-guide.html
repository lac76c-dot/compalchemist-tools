⚑ The Golden Rule of This Guide

Your role as a manager is not to manage the leave — that's HR's job. Your role is to manage the relationship. Every section of this guide reflects that principle.

01
The Disclosure Moment

When an Employee Tells You They're Struggling

Canada & US

This is often the hardest moment. An employee pulls you aside — or sends a hesitant message — and tells you something is wrong with their health. They may be emotional, uncertain, or visibly frightened. How you respond in the next five minutes shapes everything that follows: their trust in you, their willingness to engage with the leave process, and their eventual return.

You don't need to have answers. You don't need to understand the diagnosis. You need to make the person feel heard, safe, and supported — and then let HR take it from there.

🔑
You are not the diagnostician, the benefits administrator, or the accommodation approver. You are the human bridge between this employee and the support systems that exist to help them. Be that bridge — warmly, and without overstepping.

What to Say: Starter Scripts

When they first disclose
"Thank you for trusting me with this. I want you to know I'm here for you. You don't have to have all the answers right now — let's figure this out together, and I'll connect you with HR so we can make sure you have everything you need."
If they become emotional
"Take your time. There's no rush. I want to make sure you feel comfortable, and nothing about this conversation has to be solved today."
If they ask "what happens now?"
"The next step is connecting with HR — they'll walk you through your options and what the leave process looks like. I'll reach out to them today on your behalf, if that works for you. You won't have to navigate this alone."
✓
Say This
  • "Thank you for telling me."
  • "Your health comes first."
  • "Take the time you need."
  • "I'll connect you with HR — they're there to help."
  • "Your job will be here when you're ready."
  • "You don't have to share more than you're comfortable with."
✗
Avoid This
  • "What exactly is wrong with you?"
  • "How long do you think you'll be off?"
  • "This is really bad timing."
  • "Can you train someone before you go?"
  • "Have you tried [treatment/remedy]?"
  • "I had something similar — here's what I did."
🔒 Privacy First — Canada & US

You are not entitled to a diagnosis. In Canada, human rights legislation and provincial privacy laws protect medical information. In the US, the ADA and HIPAA create similar protections. Your employee only needs to tell you enough to trigger the leave process. Asking for more creates liability. When in doubt: ask less, support more.

02
Leave Initiation

Starting the Leave Process: Your Role vs. HR's Role

Canada United States

Once an employee discloses a health issue that may require time away, the clock starts. There are things you must do, things HR must do, and — critically — things you should stay out of entirely. This section maps that clearly.

✅ Manager's Responsibility
  • Notify HR promptly — same day if possible
  • Treat the disclosure as confidential
  • Ensure the employee knows HR is their point of contact
  • Document the date of disclosure (for your records only)
  • Begin thinking about operational coverage — quietly
  • Communicate to your team only what is necessary (absence, not diagnosis)
🏢 HR's Responsibility
  • Provide the employee with leave paperwork and options
  • Determine applicable leave type (medical, STD, FMLA/provincial)
  • Coordinate with benefits/payroll for income continuation
  • Obtain medical documentation (where legally permissible)
  • Track leave duration and maintain the employee file
  • Manage accommodation documentation upon return

Your Action Checklist: First 48 Hours

1
Contact HR the same day
Send a brief, factual message. Don't include diagnosis details — just let HR know an employee has disclosed a health situation that may require leave and needs to connect with them. Let them lead from here.
2
Confirm the employee has HR's contact information
Don't assume they know who to call or what to do. Provide the name, email, and/or phone number for your HR contact or leave administrator. Ask if they'd like you to make a warm introduction.
3
Address urgent operational needs — internally, quietly
Review current workload and identify what's most time-sensitive. Begin thinking about interim coverage or redistribution. Do not mention the reason for the upcoming absence to colleagues at this stage.
4
Agree on what the employee wants to tell the team
This is their story to tell. Ask: "What would you feel comfortable with me sharing with the team?" Respect their answer completely. A simple "She'll be taking some personal time off" is often enough.
5
Set a brief check-in with the employee before leave begins
Before they go, have a short conversation to confirm: transition notes, who to contact in their absence, and how they'd like to be acknowledged on their first day back. Keep it practical and warm.
⚠️ A Note on Jurisdiction

In Canada, medical leaves are governed by a mix of provincial employment standards (e.g., Ontario's ESA, BC's Employment Standards Act) and federal law for federally regulated workplaces. In the United States, FMLA applies to employers with 50+ employees, but state laws (like California's CFRA) may provide broader rights. HR will navigate this for you. Your job is not to know the law — it's to refer quickly and stay in your lane.

03
During the Leave

Staying Connected Without Crossing Boundaries

Canada & US

During leave, the instinct to "check in" can come from a genuinely caring place — but without the right guardrails, it can feel like surveillance, create pressure to return early, or expose the company to legal risk. The model recommended here is simple: HR leads all formal communication. The manager plays a warm, secondary role.

📋 The HR-Led Communication Model
HR's Role
All formal check-ins, leave status updates, paperwork, accommodation discussions, and return planning conversations.
Manager's Role
One brief personal (non-work) message early in the leave. One near the anticipated return. Nothing in between unless the employee initiates.
The Rule
If it's about work — workload, projects, teammates, timelines — it should not come from you. Route it through HR or wait until return.

What "Personal" Contact Looks Like

Early in the leave (Week 1–2)
"Hi [Name], I just wanted to reach out to let you know we're thinking of you. You don't need to respond to this — I just wanted you to hear that you're missed and that there's no pressure from our side. Take care of yourself."
Near the anticipated return date (HR will advise timing)
"Hi [Name], HR mentioned your return is coming up soon. I wanted to check in personally to say we're looking forward to having you back, and we'll make sure the transition feels comfortable. HR will be in touch about the details — I just wanted you to hear it from me too."
✓
Appropriate Contact
  • A brief, warm personal message (no reply expected)
  • A birthday or holiday acknowledgment if culturally appropriate
  • Responding to contact the employee initiates
  • Coordinating with HR if you're asked to participate in a return plan
✗
Over-Reach to Avoid
  • Sending work updates, project status, or task reminders
  • Asking how treatment is going or when they'll be back
  • Copying them on team emails "just to keep them in the loop"
  • Contacting them more than twice during a leave
  • Reaching out because a project is struggling without them
💡 The Pressure Test

Before sending any message, ask yourself: "Could this make the employee feel guilty about being off, or pressure them to return sooner?" If the answer is yes — or even maybe — don't send it. Route it through HR instead, or hold it until they're back.

Managing Your Team During the Absence

What to Tell Your Team
  • That a team member is on a leave of absence
  • The general timeframe (if known)
  • Who is covering key responsibilities in the interim
  • That questions about the leave should be directed to you — not the employee
What NOT to Tell Your Team
  • The reason for the leave (medical or otherwise)
  • Any details about the employee's health or diagnosis
  • Speculation about the duration or prognosis
  • Any hint that the leave is burdensome or inconvenient
04
Return-to-Work Planning

Welcoming Someone Back With Confidence and Care

Canada & US

Return to work is not a single moment — it's a transition. Done well, it rebuilds confidence, restores belonging, and sets the employee up for long-term success. Done poorly, it can undo months of recovery or trigger a second leave. Your job is to make the return feel normal, not ceremonial, and never awkward.

🤝
HR owns the return-to-work plan. They'll confirm the official return date, coordinate any accommodations, and prepare any documentation. Your role is to make the employee feel genuinely welcomed — not interrogated, not pitied, and not overwhelmed on day one.

Before They Return: Your Preparation Checklist

1
Work with HR on any accommodation requirements
Accommodations might include a phased return schedule, modified duties, adjusted hours, ergonomic changes, or remote work flexibility. HR will formalize these — you execute them and ensure the team respects them, quietly.
2
Brief your team — privately and briefly
Let the team know [Name] is returning. Remind them not to ask about the leave, and to be warm and low-key about the return. No balloons, no big announcements. Just normal.
3
Prepare a first-week plan
Map out a reduced or graduated workload for week one. Avoid scheduling any high-stakes meetings, performance conversations, or new projects in the first few days back. Give them space to re-acclimate.
4
Schedule a private 1:1 for Day 1
Keep it to 20–30 minutes. This meeting is about them, not about work. Ask how they're feeling, what they need from you, and what would make the transition easiest. Then listen.
Your Day 1 opening statement
"Welcome back — it's genuinely good to see you. Today is just about getting you comfortable and settled in. There's no pressure to hit the ground running. What would be most helpful for you today?"
If they ask about what they missed
"There's a lot to catch up on, and we'll make sure you get there at a pace that works for you. I've put together a brief summary — let's go through it together whenever you're ready. There's no rush."
✓
Return Week Wins
  • Check in briefly each day during the first week
  • Honour any accommodation plan — fully, without commentary
  • Let them set the pace for workload ramp-up
  • Treat them exactly as you would any returning team member
  • Ask "what do you need?" not "when will you be at full capacity?"
✗
Return Week Pitfalls
  • Overloading them with everything that happened while they were gone
  • Asking about their health, treatment, or prognosis
  • Making comments about how hard things were without them
  • Announcing their return to the whole team in a public way
  • Skipping the accommodation plan "just this once"
🌱 Graduated Returns Are Common

Both Canadian and US frameworks support phased or graduated return-to-work arrangements. This might mean starting at 50% hours, avoiding specific physical tasks, or working remotely for an initial period. These are not concessions — they are tools for a successful, lasting return. Employees who return on a supported plan are significantly more likely to stay well and remain employed long-term.

05
What Goes Wrong

Common Manager Mistakes — and How to Fix Them

Most managers don't make mistakes out of malice — they make them out of discomfort, operational pressure, or the genuine desire to "stay helpful." Here are the patterns we see most often, and what to do instead.

❌ The Mistake
✅ The Fix
1.Asking too many questions at disclosure. The manager, trying to "plan ahead," asks about the diagnosis, the expected duration, and whether the employee can train a replacement first.
Express support, get HR involved immediately. You don't need operational information before HR has even spoken to the employee. Ask HR to loop you in on what you need to know for coverage.
2.Over-communicating during leave. Weekly "just checking in" messages, CC'ing the employee on team emails, or sharing project updates "so they don't fall behind."
Follow the two-message rule: once early, once near return. Anything else should come from HR or wait. When in doubt, ask yourself: "Would this make them feel pressured?"
3.Telling the team too much. Out of a desire to explain the absence or build empathy, the manager shares details about the health condition.
Confirm with the employee what they want shared — then share exactly that and nothing more. "Personal medical leave" is enough. Privacy violations create liability and destroy trust.
4.Making the return feel like a performance review. The manager uses Day 1 to discuss missed deliverables, performance gaps, or expectations going forward.
The first day back is a welcome, not an assessment. Keep Day 1 light. Schedule a proper reintegration meeting for Week 2, after the employee has re-acclimated.
5.Quietly reassigning responsibilities permanently. The manager moves key projects or promotions while the employee is off, and doesn't restore them upon return.
Any structural changes made during leave should be reviewed for fairness before the employee returns. Consult HR. In both Canada and the US, adverse treatment during or following a leave can constitute discrimination.
6.Treating the employee as fragile. Overprotecting the returning employee — not giving feedback, excluding them from decisions, or tiptoeing around them indefinitely.
Return to normal management as quickly as the accommodation plan allows. Treating someone as fragile communicates that you don't believe in their recovery. Respect their resilience.
7.Going off-script out of compassion. The manager, wanting to help, promises things HR hasn't approved — extended timelines, flexible arrangements, or "don't worry about the paperwork."
All commitments related to the leave, accommodations, or return plan must come from or be approved by HR. Your kindness can create unintended obligations. Always say: "Let me check with HR and confirm that for you."
✦ The Underlying Theme

Almost every mistake on this list comes from the same root cause: the manager trying to do more than their role requires. The best managers in this situation are the ones who understand that doing less — but doing it with genuine warmth — is actually the highest-quality response.

Quick Reference

Manager's One-Page Cheat Sheet

📌 Your Role at Each Stage
At Disclosure
Listen. Thank. Reassure. Contact HR same day. Keep it confidential. Don't ask for details.
Initiating Leave
Notify HR. Confirm employee has HR contact. Plan quiet operational coverage. Agree on team messaging.
During Leave
Two messages max. Nothing work-related. HR leads all formal contact. Protect the employee's privacy with your team.
Return Planning
Work with HR on accommodations. Prepare the team quietly. Reduce Week 1 workload. Schedule a private Day 1 check-in.
Day 1 Back
"Welcome back. What do you need?" Keep it warm, low-key, and entirely about them. No work reviews. No big announcements.
The Mistake to Avoid
Doing too much. Over-communicating, over-explaining, over-promising. Refer to HR. Be human. Stay in your lane.
⚠️ Always Involve HR — No Exceptions

This guide is a practical resource, not a legal compliance tool. Leave laws in Canada and the US are complex, vary by province/state and employer size, and change regularly. Your HR team is your single source of truth on what's required, what's permissible, and what protects both the employee and the organization. When in doubt, ask HR first.
