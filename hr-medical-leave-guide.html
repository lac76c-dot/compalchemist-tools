⚑ The HR Philosophy Behind This Guide

Process protects people. A well-run leave isn't just a compliance exercise — it's one of the most meaningful things HR does. The manager may be the employee's daily anchor, but HR is the architecture underneath. This guide helps you be both structurally sound and genuinely human at every stage.

01
Phase One · Intake & Triage

The First 24 Hours After Notification

Canada & US

The notification arrives — from a manager, from the employee directly, or sometimes from payroll flagging an unplanned absence. However it lands, your first response sets the entire tone of the leave. This phase is about making contact, gathering only what you need, and ensuring the employee knows they're in capable hands.

🧭
Your job in the first 24 hours is not to solve everything. It's to make the employee feel safe, confirm HR is their point of contact, gather the minimum necessary information, and begin the formal process. Competence communicated calmly is the most reassuring thing you can offer right now.

Intake Checklist — First 24 Hours

Acknowledge receipt of the notification to the manager (or employee, if they contacted HR directly) — same dayA brief, warm reply confirming you've received the information and will be in touch shortly.
Make direct contact with the employeeA phone call is preferred over email for the first touch. Warmth travels better over voice.
Gather the minimum necessary informationAnticipated start date, anticipated duration (if known), and whether the employee has a treating healthcare provider. Nothing more.
Confirm the employee's preferred contact method during leaveEmail, phone, or a designated family/support contact for administrative matters only.
Identify the applicable leave type(s) — provisionallySee Section 2 for full determination. At intake, a preliminary categorization is enough.
Brief the manager on their role and boundariesConfirm HR is taking the lead. Share the two-message rule for manager contact during leave.
Open a leave file or record in your HRISDate of notification, employee name, leave type, anticipated duration. This is your audit trail.

Your First Call with the Employee: Scripts

Opening the conversation
"Hi [Name], this is [Your Name] from HR. I heard from [Manager] that you're going through something difficult right now, and I wanted to reach out personally. You don't need to have everything figured out — that's what I'm here for. Can you tell me a little about where things are at?"
When they're unsure how long they'll be off
"That's completely okay — we don't need an exact date right now. Your focus should be on getting well. We'll plan for what we know and adjust as things become clearer. There's a process for that, and I'll walk you through each step."
Closing the intake call
"Here's what happens next: I'm going to send you a summary email outlining the leave process and any forms you'll need to complete. Your manager knows that I'll be your main point of contact from here. You can reach me at [contact info] anytime. How are you feeling about all of this?"
✓
HR Best Practice
  • Lead with empathy before process
  • Confirm you — not the manager — are the point of contact
  • Ask only what's needed to start the paperwork
  • Send a follow-up summary email within the same day
  • Document date, time, and summary of the call
✗
Common Intake Errors
  • Immediately asking for a diagnosis or medical details
  • Sending a form-heavy email before making human contact
  • Letting the manager remain the primary contact by default
  • Failing to document the intake conversation
  • Treating the call as a screening exercise rather than a welcome
🌿 The Human Note

Employees on the verge of a medical leave are often scared — about their health, their job security, their team, their finances. Your first call is not administrative. It is the moment they decide whether this organization is safe to be vulnerable with. Answer that question well, and everything that follows is easier.

02
Phase Two · Leave Setup

Establishing the Leave: Type, Documentation & Jurisdiction

Canada United States

Once intake is complete, your next task is to determine the correct leave framework, collect documentation within legal limits, coordinate with payroll and benefits, and formalize the leave in writing. This is where process does the heavy lifting — and where precision prevents problems later.

Step 1: Determine Leave Type

Canada
  • Personal Emergency / Medical Leave — provincial ESA entitlements (unpaid, job-protected; varies by province)
  • Short-Term Disability (STD) — employer-provided or insured plan; typically weeks 1–17
  • Long-Term Disability (LTD) — insurer-managed; typically after 17 weeks STD
  • EI Sickness Benefits — federal; up to 26 weeks; employee-applied
  • Human Rights Accommodation Leave — duty to accommodate to undue hardship
  • Federal Canada Labour Code — applies to federally regulated workplaces only
United States
  • FMLA — up to 12 weeks unpaid, job-protected; 50+ employee threshold
  • ADA Leave — reasonable accommodation; may extend beyond FMLA
  • STD / LTD — employer or insurer plan; runs concurrently with FMLA where applicable
  • State Leave Laws — CFRA (CA), NY PFL, WA PFML and others may exceed FMLA
  • Workers' Compensation — for work-related illness/injury; separate process
  • Company Policy Leave — discretionary extensions beyond statutory minimums
⚠️ Stacking Rules

Multiple leave types can apply simultaneously. In Canada, STD and provincial job-protection leave typically run concurrently. In the US, FMLA and ADA leave can overlap — but FMLA exhaustion does not end the ADA accommodation obligation. Always assess all applicable frameworks before closing a leave file. When in doubt, consult legal counsel or your insurer's case manager.

Step 2: Documentation — What You Can and Cannot Request

✅ You May Request
  • Confirmation that a medical condition exists
  • The anticipated start and expected duration of leave
  • Functional limitations or restrictions (for accommodation planning)
  • Healthcare provider's name and specialty (for STD/LTD insurer)
  • Completed insurer or company leave forms (signed by treating provider)
  • Fitness-for-return confirmation before the employee resumes duties
🚫 You May Not Request
  • The specific diagnosis or medical condition
  • Details about treatment, medications, or therapy
  • Prognosis or long-term health outlook (beyond functional capacity)
  • Access to medical records directly from the treating provider
  • Second opinions without clear business justification and legal footing
  • Any information that would reveal a protected characteristic

Step 3: Payroll, Benefits & Administration Checklist

Notify payroll of the leave start date and pay continuation rulesConfirm whether STD/LTD offsets apply to base salary, and when payroll transitions from regular to leave pay.
Confirm benefit continuation during leaveIn Canada, group benefits typically continue during job-protected leaves if premiums are maintained. Confirm with your plan administrator. In the US, confirm COBRA eligibility/timing during FMLA/non-FMLA periods.
Initiate STD/LTD claim with insurer (if applicable)Provide the employee with insurer contact info and claim forms. Note: the insurer manages the claim; HR manages the employment relationship.
Issue a formal leave confirmation letter to the employeeConfirming: leave type, start date, anticipated end date (if known), benefit continuation status, and return-to-work process.
Update HRIS with leave status, type, and key datesThis is your compliance audit trail. Gaps in leave records create liability.
Brief the manager — in writing — on their roleConfirm HR leads all formal communication. Provide the two-message guideline for personal contact during leave.
Leave confirmation letter opening paragraph
"This letter confirms your approved medical leave of absence, effective [date]. We want to ensure this period is as straightforward as possible for you. Below you'll find the details of your leave, including benefit continuation and what to expect as your return date approaches. Please don't hesitate to reach out — I'm your primary contact throughout."
03
Phase Three · Active Leave

Managing the Active Leave — Communication, File & Extensions

Canada & US

Once the leave is formally established, HR's role shifts from setup to stewardship. This phase is less visible but equally important. A well-managed active leave means fewer complications at return, better compliance documentation, and an employee who feels held — not forgotten — by their organization.

📋 HR Communication Model During Active Leave
HR-Initiated Contact
Administrative only: documentation reminders, benefit updates, leave extensions, return date planning. Never clinical or casual.
Cadence
Short leaves (<4 weeks): one mid-leave check-in. Longer leaves: monthly administrative touchpoint. Always by the employee's preferred method.
What HR Never Asks
How treatment is going, whether they feel better, or when they think they'll be ready to return — unless they raise it first.

Mid-Leave Administrative Check-In

Mid-leave HR check-in (email or call)
"Hi [Name], I wanted to touch base to confirm that everything is in order on our end — your benefits are active and your leave is properly recorded. I also want to make sure you have everything you need from us. Your anticipated return date is [date]. As that approaches, I'll be in touch to start the return-planning conversation. Please reach out at any time if anything changes or if you need support navigating anything."

File Maintenance During Active Leave

Update HRIS if leave is extended beyond the original anticipated end dateDocument the new anticipated return date and the reason for extension (e.g., medical certification of continued disability).
Track leave duration against applicable statutory entitlementsFlag when an employee is approaching the end of job-protected leave. This requires advance planning, not a last-minute call.
Maintain insurer / STD-LTD communication recordsKeep copies of all insurer correspondence in the leave file. The insurer manages the claim; HR manages the employment record.
Flag any performance management processes that were underway before the leaveThese must be paused. Continuing a PIP or disciplinary process during a medical leave creates significant legal exposure.
Ensure interim coverage decisions are documented and not permanentRole backfill is operational, not a restructure. The position must be held for the employee's return unless a formal consultation/restructuring process is conducted.

When a Leave Extends Beyond the Expected Date

1
Request updated medical certification
Send a formal, brief request to the employee asking for updated documentation confirming continued disability and a revised anticipated return date. Give a reasonable deadline — typically 5–7 business days.
2
Assess remaining statutory entitlements
Determine how much job-protected leave remains. If the employee is approaching the end of statutory entitlement, initiate a legal review before making any employment decisions. This is not a unilateral HR call.
3
Issue a written leave extension confirmation
Even if no decision is required, document the extension in writing to the employee — confirming the new anticipated return date, benefit status, and that HR remains their point of contact.
4
Brief the manager on operational implications
Not the medical facts — the timeline. A manager who doesn't know a leave has extended will fill the gap with assumptions. A brief, factual update prevents that.
🌿 The Human Note

Extended leaves are often accompanied by guilt. Employees worry they're letting the team down, that their job isn't safe, or that they've become a burden. A single sentence at the end of an administrative check-in — "Your job is here. Focus on what matters right now." — costs nothing and means everything.

04
Phase Four · Return Planning

Building the Bridge Back — Return-to-Work Planning

Canada & US

Return-to-work planning should begin at least two to four weeks before the anticipated return date — not the day before. This is one of the most operationally complex phases of the leave lifecycle, and the one most likely to go wrong when left to the last minute. HR owns this phase entirely.

📐
A return-to-work plan is not a formality. It is the document that determines whether the employee's return is sustainable. In both Canada and the US, HR has a legal obligation to assess accommodation needs before a return. Getting this right protects the employee, the manager, and the organization.

Return-to-Work Planning Timeline

4 WEEKS OUT
Initiate Contact
Reach out to the employee to confirm anticipated return date and begin the conversation about what they'll need.
3 WEEKS OUT
Request RTW Cert.
Request a fitness-for-return form from the treating provider outlining any functional limitations or restrictions.
2 WEEKS OUT
Draft RTW Plan
Review medical documentation. Draft accommodation or graduated return plan. Loop in the manager for operational input.
1 WEEK OUT
Confirm & Finalize
Share the finalized plan with the employee. Confirm they agree. Brief manager. Confirm any physical or system changes needed.
DAY BEFORE
Final Check-In
A brief, warm message: "Everything is set. We're looking forward to seeing you tomorrow. I'll check in with you on Day 1."

Accommodation Assessment: What HR Needs to Evaluate

Physical / Ergonomic
  • Workstation modifications
  • Reduced physical demands
  • Parking or mobility access
  • Rest periods or sit/stand options
Scheduling / Hours
  • Graduated return (e.g., 50% → 75% → 100%)
  • Modified start/end times
  • Reduced hours (temporary)
  • Remote work flexibility
Duties / Role
  • Temporary modification of specific tasks
  • Reduced complexity in first weeks
  • Avoidance of specific stressors
  • Alternate role (if permanent restrictions require)
Opening the RTW conversation with the employee
"We're really glad your return is coming up. Before we finalize everything, I want to make sure we get the plan right for you. Can you tell me how you're feeling about coming back, and whether there's anything about the role or environment that you're thinking about?"
Briefing the manager on the accommodation plan
"[Employee] is returning on [date] with a few temporary adjustments to support their transition. I'll send you a summary in writing. The most important thing right now is to honour the plan exactly as written — not to be hard on yourself about adjusting, just to follow it. I'll check in with both of you after week one."
⚠️ The Duty to Accommodate (Canada) / Reasonable Accommodation (US)

In Canada, the duty to accommodate extends to the point of undue hardship — a high threshold that considers financial cost, health and safety impact, and organizational disruption. In the US, the ADA requires reasonable accommodation absent undue hardship. Neither is a "we tried" standard. Both require a genuine, documented, individualized assessment process. If you're uncertain, get legal advice before declining an accommodation request.

05
Phase Five · Reintegration

The First Week Back — HR's Role in Reintegration

Canada & US

The return-to-work plan was the architecture. The first week is the lived experience. HR's role shifts from planner to monitor — ensuring the accommodation plan is being followed, the employee feels supported, and any early warning signs are caught before they become a second leave.

HR's Week-One Checklist

Day 1: Brief check-in with the employeeBy phone or in person. Warm and brief — "How did today feel? Anything we need to adjust?" This is a temperature check, not a debrief.
Day 1: Confirm with the manager that the accommodation plan is in placeA quick message: "How did today go? Anything to flag?" Managers sometimes default to old routines — this check-in catches that early.
Day 3: Second brief check-in with the employeeMid-week is when the fatigue of re-entry often sets in. Catching it here allows for adjustment before it escalates.
End of Week 1: Formal check-in with both the employee and manager separatelyAssess: Is the plan working? Does anything need adjustment? Are there any interpersonal dynamics to address? Document your notes.
Update HRIS to reflect return to active statusClose the leave record, confirm accommodation plan start date, and note the end date of any graduated return schedule.

Early Warning Signs to Watch For

✓
Healthy Reintegration Signs
  • Employee appears engaged and present
  • Accommodation plan is being followed by the team
  • Manager reports positive interactions
  • Employee raises minor adjustments proactively
  • Attendance is consistent with the return plan
✗
Flags That Need HR Action
  • Employee seems overwhelmed, tearful, or withdrawn
  • Manager is bypassing or minimizing accommodations
  • Absenteeism patterns emerging in Week 1 or 2
  • Employee reports feeling pressured to perform at full capacity
  • Team dynamics are tense around the return
End-of-week-one check-in with employee
"I just wanted to check in now that you've got your first week behind you. I know re-entry can be a lot, even when things go well. How are you feeling about how the week went? Is there anything about the plan that needs to be adjusted, or anything you want me to be aware of going into next week?"
🌿 The Human Note

The first week back is often the most disorienting of the entire leave experience — not because people aren't ready, but because re-entry requires adjusting to a rhythm they've been away from. Your check-ins this week are not administrative. They're the signal that the organization meant what it said during leave: we've got you.

06
Phase Six · Follow-Up & Closure

Post-Return Follow-Up & File Closure

Canada & US

The leave doesn't end when the employee returns. In the 30–90 days that follow, HR plays a quiet but critical role: monitoring the reintegration, escalating when needed, transitioning the employee back to normal performance management frameworks, and closing the file with proper documentation.

30 / 60 / 90-Day Follow-Up Framework

30 DAYS
Accommodation Review
Is the plan still appropriate? Check in with both employee and manager. Adjust if needed. Document any changes.
60 DAYS
Graduated Ramp-Up
If on a phased return, confirm progress toward full duties. Any concerns get addressed now — not at 90 days.
90 DAYS
Full Integration Review
Is the employee fully reintegrated? Are accommodations still needed or can they be formally closed? Document the outcome.

Escalation Triggers: When HR Must Act

!
Attendance deterioration post-return
Frequent absences within the first 90 days of return may signal that the accommodation plan is insufficient, that the workplace environment is not supportive, or that the employee is not yet medically ready. Address this with the employee directly before involving the manager.
!
Manager non-compliance with the accommodation plan
This is a serious issue. Non-compliance exposes the organization to human rights liability in both Canada and the US. Address it directly with the manager and their leader. Document your intervention.
!
Employee raises concerns about retaliation or adverse treatment
Any indication that an employee is being penalized — formally or informally — for having taken a medical leave requires immediate HR investigation. This is not a "monitor and see" situation.
!
Second leave within 6 months of return
Treat a second leave as a signal that either the original accommodation was insufficient or that there's an underlying workplace factor contributing to the employee's health challenges. Begin a structured accommodation review rather than simply processing the leave again.

Closing the Leave File — Documentation Checklist

Confirmed return-to-work date recorded in HRIS
Fitness-for-return medical certification on file
Accommodation plan (if applicable) — signed by employee, manager, and HR
All leave correspondence (letters, emails, forms) filed in the leave record
Insurer or STD/LTD file closed or transitioned as applicable
Performance management process (if previously paused) formally re-initiated with HR guidance
30/60/90-day follow-up outcomes documented
Leave file retained per applicable records retention requirementsCanada: varies by province, generally 3–7 years. US: FMLA records must be retained for 3 years minimum.
✦ The Closing Thought

A medical leave handled well is one of the most powerful retention tools an organization has. The employee who returns to a job that was held for them, supported during their absence, and thoughtfully re-integrated is far more likely to remain — and to speak well of the organization. HR doesn't just manage the process. HR shapes the experience. Make it one worth remembering.

⚠️ Always Seek Legal Advice When

You are considering denying or ending a leave. An employee requests an accommodation you believe is unreasonable. You need to fill the role permanently during a protected leave. A performance or discipline process intersects with a medical leave. An employee alleges retaliation. HR expertise and legal expertise are not the same thing. Know when you need both.
